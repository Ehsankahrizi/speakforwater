## TL;DR

The GRACE-FO satellite mission has been measuring tiny variations in Earth's
gravitational pull for nearly a decade. New analysis shows that groundwater
depletion in major aquifers is now significant enough to detect from orbit —
and it's accelerating in regions that feed a third of the world's population.

## Key findings

- Major aquifers in California's Central Valley, the North China Plain, and
  northwestern India lost an average of 22 cubic kilometers of water per year
  during the 2018–2024 study period.
- The rate of loss has accelerated in 8 of the 10 most-stressed aquifers
  compared to the previous GRACE mission (2002–2017).
- Loss correlates strongly with irrigated agriculture but is now also driven
  by drought-induced reductions in recharge — a feedback loop with climate.

## Why it matters

Groundwater is the bank account that gets withdrawn from in droughts. When
this account empties, surface water disappears too — wells run dry, rivers
shrink, and farmland becomes uneconomical. The paper's authors argue that
without satellite-based monitoring, most countries would be unable to detect
the depletion happening beneath their feet.

## For Iran and the Middle East

The paper highlights the Tigris-Euphrates basin and Iran's central plateau as
two of the fastest-depleting regions globally. Khuzestan, Isfahan, and
Khorasan have lost groundwater at rates that exceed the global aquifer-stress
threshold by factors of 3–5×.
