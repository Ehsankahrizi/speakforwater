## TL;DR

A meta-analysis of 247 studies across 56 countries finds microplastics in 83%
of tested tap water samples and 92% of bottled water. Concentrations vary by
4 orders of magnitude. The health implications are uncertain but the data
suggest current treatment infrastructure is largely ineffective.

## Key findings

- Median tap water concentration: 0.4 particles per liter (range: 0–10,000).
- Bottled water averaged 28× higher concentrations than tap water.
- Treatment efficiency varies wildly: granular activated carbon removes ~85%,
  while basic chlorination achieves only ~30%.
- Particle size distribution is bimodal — most particles are either >100 μm
  (filterable) or <10 μm (largely impossible to remove with conventional methods).

## What's still unclear

The paper is careful to distinguish what we know from what we don't:

> "The presence of microplastics in drinking water is well-documented; the
> health impact at typical exposure levels is not."

In-vitro studies show inflammatory responses at high doses, but extrapolating
to the dose levels people actually consume requires assumptions the data don't
support yet. The authors call for standardized measurement protocols (current
studies use 12 different sampling methods) before regulatory action.

## What you can do

The paper's most actionable finding: switching from bottled to filtered tap
water reduces microplastic exposure by an order of magnitude. The cheapest
effective filter is a standard NSF/ANSI 401 carbon block.
